# Safety Notes

Yeah. Here’s a rough **BCS legal/safety language pack** you could paste into notes and later refine.

FDA says animal devices generally do **not** require 510(k), PMA, or premarket approval, but FDA can still act if an animal device is misbranded or adulterated. FTC also cares about express and implied health/safety claims, especially claims that need substantiation. So the theme is: **literal features, no guaranteed outcomes, owner stays responsible.** ([U.S. Food and Drug Administration][1])

## Short product description

> BCS is an owner-controlled remote feeding and observation system. It lets owners view their pet, dispense owner-loaded food or soft treats, and review camera-confirmed feeding events from their phone.

## Diabetes-adjacent but careful version

> Some pets use small, frequent feeding routines developed by their owners and veterinarians. BCS helps owners remotely dispense food or soft treats, view the feeding area, and keep a timestamped record of care events.

## Stronger diabetic-pet version

> Some owners of diabetic pets use small, frequent meals and close observation as part of routines they develop with their veterinarian. BCS helps owners remotely dispense food or soft treats and verify events by camera. BCS does not diagnose, treat, prevent, or manage diabetes or any other medical condition.

## Main safety line

> BCS helps you monitor and respond. It does not replace your alertness, judgment, veterinarian’s guidance, or in-person care.

## “Not responsible for blind reliance” version

> Do not rely on BCS as the sole means of monitoring, feeding, treating, or caring for any pet, especially a medically fragile pet. Power, network, camera, app, dispenser, sensor, mechanical, user, and animal-behavior failures can occur.

## No guarantee language

> BCS cannot guarantee that food or treats are dispensed, that your pet eats, that your pet is safe, or that an emergency will be prevented.

## Verification language

> BCS reports what it can observe. A dispense command, a dispense attempt, visible food, and observed eating are separate events. If BCS cannot verify an event, treat that event as unverified.

## App status language

Use labels like:

> Command sent
> Device online
> Camera available
> Dispense attempted
> Food visible
> Eating observed
> Eating not confirmed
> Camera unavailable
> Dispense unverified
> Connection lost
> Routine failed
> In-person check recommended

Avoid labels like:

> Dog safe
> Emergency handled
> Rescue complete
> Hypoglycemia prevented
> Treatment successful

## Fallback routine warning

> Fallback routines are user-configured convenience features. They may occur at the wrong time, fail to occur, or be unverified. Do not rely on fallback routines as emergency protection.

## Connection-loss routine description

> If the device loses connection for a user-selected period, BCS can run a user-configured routine, such as taking a snapshot, attempting a food or soft-treat dispense, and recording the event. The owner remains responsible for checking status and deciding when in-person care is needed.

## External data / Libre-ish language

> External alerts and data sources, if configured, are user-provided inputs for convenience. BCS does not interpret glucose data, recommend treatment, recommend insulin dosing, or determine whether food or treats are medically needed.

## Soft treat / honey-ish language

> The soft-treat module dispenses owner-loaded edible material. Owners are responsible for selecting pet-safe contents, amounts, and timing.

## Setup checkbox

> I understand that BCS can fail due to power loss, network loss, camera failure, app failure, dispenser jam, user error, setup error, or pet behavior. I will not rely on BCS as the only way to monitor or care for my pet.

## Advanced feature checkbox

> I understand that automated, scheduled, or fallback dispensing may occur at the wrong time, fail to occur, or be unverified. I remain responsible for checking my pet and deciding when in-person care is needed.

## Terms-ish paragraph

> BCS is provided as a remote feeding, soft-treat dispensing, observation, and logging tool. It is not a veterinary medical device, diagnostic tool, treatment system, life-support system, emergency-response system, or substitute for professional veterinary care or in-person supervision. Use of BCS is at the owner’s discretion and risk. Owners are solely responsible for their pet’s care decisions, feeding routines, food/treat selection, monitoring, and emergency response.

## One blunt line for high-risk screens

> If your pet may be in danger, do not wait for BCS. Check your pet directly or arrange in-person care.

## The most reusable line

> **BCS gives you more visibility and more options from a distance. It does not make your pet safe by itself.**

That last one is probably the whole philosophy.
